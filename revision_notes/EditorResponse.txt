Editor comments:

Thank you for considering PNAS Nexus as an outlet for your work. We have solicited and now received two in-depth reviews of your paper.

Both of the two reviewers found the overall quality of this paper was appreciable. Yet, in order to meet the criteria of publishing at PNAS Nexus, several critical revisions are needed. Given that the issues raised by the review team are rather serious, an ultimate acceptance cannot be guaranteed, though I do encourage you to submit a revised version to address all the questions raised by the reviewers. I summarize the core issues below.

First, the review team suggested you reorganized the introduction of the manuscript so as to further clarify the major focus of your research.

Second, the review team raised questions the different findings from prior research (e.g., Dion et al., 2018) and you may also need to address the issue regarding whether teachers can have freedom to choose whether to engage in team teaching, the incentives, and how all these may affect your findings.

Third, research methodology and interpretation of the results. Please follow the suggestions from the review team to address issues related to research methodology, including but not limited to use of controls, lack of representativeness of your data coverage, missing data, and measures (e.g., novelty). Please also pay attention to presentation and interpretation of your findings (e.g., temporal changes seen in the graphs). Would you be able to get more data and see the influence of COVID 19 on the findings? The review team also requested you run additional analyses (e.g., US versus non-US and by subject areas such as natural sciences, humanities, social sciences).

Comments for the Author:
In this study, the authors analyze 2.9 million syllabi across 15 countries from 1990 to 2019 to assess how the instructors’ sex (individually or in teams) influences the degree of interdisciplinarity, the average age of assigned readings, and the percentage of female authors included in the syllabus. A linear mixed effects model shows that female teams and mixed gender teams assign more work by women scholars and that the interdisciplinarity range is higher in these instances. Female instructors/teams tend to assign newer work as well. The study leverages an interesting dataset and uses a clever research design including 1) text analysis (bag of words) to capture a syllabus’ level of interdisciplinarity and 2) a Monte Carlo randomization approach to assess actual gender distributions of teaching teams and what is expected based on institutional data. The study also provides insights into how team-teaching influences syllabus content, especially in terms of the sex of the teams (MM, MF, FF). The findings will be of interest to readers of the journal.

The finding that female teaching teams tend to include more readings by female authors accords with findings from the citation literature. See for example: Dion, Michelle L., Jane Lawrence Sumner, and Sara McLaughlin Mitchell. 2018. “Gendered citation patterns across political science and social science methodology fields.” Political Analysis 26(3): 312-327. On the other hand, Dion et al do not find that mixed gender teams cite more work by women in research articles than male teams, so there is an interesting bit of difference in these results.

There are a lot of temporal changes seen in the graphs and I'm wondering what is driving them. In many cases, the expansion towards more mixed/female team teaching seems to experience a backsliding in the last few years of the data. I think the authors should try to identify what may be driving these changes. It’s unfortunate that the data end in 2019 because it would be interesting to see how the Covid-19 pandemic altered these patterns further.

There’s almost an assumption that faculty can engage in team teaching if they desire, but that is not true in many disciplines or institutions. This possibility is briefly mentioned on page 11, but I think you should discuss this more. In my institution, it would be difficult to get credit for team teaching. I teach 4 courses per year and typically any team teaching would be done as an overload beyond the 4 courses. I realize your fixed effects for institutions and countries help to pick that up, but I'm curious what the data looks like descriptively across countries.

Similarly, the data is very U.S. dominant (66% of all syllabi). What if you run a model for U.S. institutions and compare that to a model with institutions in non-U.S. countries? Are the results similar or different? Why is team teaching less common in EU countries? This is the kind of thing I wanted more discussion of and some analyses to explore these differences.

It could be interesting to run separate analyses in general areas as well (e.g., sciences, humanities, social sciences) to see if the general patterns generalize across these areas.
