Comments for the Author:

The paper uses a large and original dataset (over five million syllabi) to study how the gender composition of teaching teams is associated with interdisciplinarity, novelty of readings, and the share of female-authored material. Systematic evidence on teaching teams and the determinants of educational content in higher education is still limited, so documenting these patterns is useful and potentially important. That said, I have a small set of concerns with the analysis, which I outline below.

- My main concern is that the central correlations (teaching team gender composition and measures of content) may be spurious and specifically driven by the mechanisms used by universities to assign faculty to courses and by men and women sorting into different subfields within fields. In most universities, teaching teams are not freely formed by instructors; courses are typically assigned by chairs or administrators based on expertise and field specialization. Men and women sort into different topics within fields (see for example Chari and Goldsmith-Pinkham 2017 for economics). Suppose that a course is more multidisciplinary by design. Given that men and women sort across subfields, that course is going to be more likely to have a gender-diverse team. This does not imply that the gender diversity of the teaching team drives interdisciplinarity. Since the Monte Carlo and regression analyses operate at relatively coarse field level, this concern remains. The paper should engage more directly with this alternative explanation and clarify that the results are descriptive correlations.

- Given the size of the dataset, the empirical specification could be much richer. For example, including country × field × course level × year fixed effects would absorb more institutional and curricular heterogeneity and help address some of the sorting concerns above. In addition, more discussion is needed on how course levels are harmonized across countries. The coding rule is validated for the U.S., but it is unclear how comparable course levels are across European contexts.

- The finding that women assign more recent readings may reflect cohort effects rather than gender per se. Female representation has increased over time, so women in the sample may be younger on average. Younger instructors may be more likely to assign recent material regardless of gender. The paper should attempt to address this possibility, for example by proxying for seniority (e.g., first appearance in the data) or by exploring whether the gender gap in recency changes over time.

- I also have concerns about the adjustment to the female-author ratio (adding one to the numerator and two to the denominator) that resemble the concern people have had about using log(1+x) to address the possibility that x can be zero. This can materially distort ratios in small reading lists. For example, consider a course with only one female author and no male authors: the true ratio is 1, but the adjusted ratio becomes 2/3. If a second female author is added (and no male authors), the true ratio remains 1, but the adjusted ratio increases to 3/4. The distortion is largest for courses with fewer authors and varies mechanically with the number of readings, which may itself be related to course and team characteristics. It would be preferable to show results using the unadjusted ratio (with appropriate safeguards for small denominators) or to use a count-based measure while flexibly controlling for total readings.

Overall, the paper documents interesting patterns, but some of the interpretation—especially around mixed-gender teams as a “lever” for interdisciplinarity or innovation—goes beyond what the design can support. A more cautious discussion of mechanisms and policy implications would strengthen the contribution.
